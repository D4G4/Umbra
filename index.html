Why it dims, not brightens

Subtracting light is the only way to slow it down.

OLED burn-in is differential sub-pixel aging — roughly proportional to how much extra light a pixel emits over time. A compositing overlay can only slow that down by subtracting luminance from static regions; adding light would make aging worse. So Umbra draws a steady, dark scrim — never lighter than the background, never blue, the fastest-aging channel.

No animation, on purpose — moving the dark layer can’t relocate the bright content beneath it, so a consistent dim lowers average luminance the most.

An honest note

The effect is real.
The magnitude is modest.

On a Tandem OLED (dual emission layer, e.g. the LG 39GX950B) the panel already halves per-layer stress, so the benefit there is small. It matters more on regular single-layer WOLED / QD-OLED. The biggest wins are still native anyway: dark-mode menu bar, auto-hiding Dock, lower panel brightness, and your display’s own pixel-shift. Treat Umbra as a modest dimming aid — not guaranteed burn-in “protection.”
